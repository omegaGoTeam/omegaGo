Jargon

This is a list of commonly used terms in Go. See also Go Terms for a more complete list. Some of these terms come from the Japanese language but we include them because they are very common among Go players.

  • Essential Terms
    • Atari. A move that reduces a stone or chain of stones to one liberty. The stones with one liberty are said to be "in atari". An atari play that reduce one's own stones' liberties to one is "self-atari"; an atari against two groups is "double atari". Stones in atari can be captured on the opponent's next turn unless they are defended.
    • Chain/string/group. A group of stones that are directly adjacent along the lines of the board. The stones in a chain share liberties and live or die as a unit.
    • Life and Death. A group that has life is a group has two eyes, lives in seki or is secure enough to survive any attack. A group is dead when its owner cannot, playing first with correct play, make it live with two eyes or in seki or make a ko for life, given accurate play by the opponent.
    • Eye. An empty space surrounded by one player's stones such that none of them can be brought into atari separately.
    • Kill. To kill a group is to ensure that the group will ultimately perish and be removed from the board.
    • Ko. A position in which single stones could be captured back and forth indefinitely were there not a rule forbidding such repetition.
    • Liberty. An empty point adjacent to a stone.
    • Point. An intersection of two lines on the go board.
    • Territory
      • 1) a region of the board that belongs to one player because it is surrounded by stones belonging to a living group, and in which the opponent cannot make a living group;
      • 2) a region which almost belongs to one player.
    • Area. A player’s area consists of that player’s territory plus all points occupied by that player’s living stones.
    • Tsumego. A life and death problem, a Go puzzle.
  • Useful Terms
    • Cut. A move which separates two or more of a player's stones by occupying a point adjacent to them.
    • Dame. A neutral point between established Black and White positions which does not count as territory for either player.
    • False Eye. An empty space surrounded by one player's stones such that at least one of them can be brought into atari separately.
    • Fuseki. The opening phase, a Japanese go term meaning arraying forces for battle; it refers to the initial phase of the game, especially before there are any weak groups.
    • Joseki. An opening, established sequences of play considered equitable for both players, especially early moves near a corner.
    • Sente.
      • 1) the initiative;
      • 2) a play that must be answered;
      • 3) a play that is answered.
    • Gote
      • 1) a move or sequence of moves that does not have to be answered;
      • 2) a move or sequence of moves that is not answered.
    • Ko threat. A threatening move played either to provoke an immediate response from the opponent, allowing the player to recapture the ko on his next move, or to make a gain if the opponent ignores it.
    • Komi. Points added to a player's score, normally given to White in compensation for Black's advantage in playing first.
    • Kikashi/forcing move. A sente move that produces a certain effect and can then be abandoned without regret.
    • Ladder. A technique for capturing stones where at each step, the attacker reduces the defender's liberties from two to one: especially an attack of this type that proceeds diagonally across the board.
    • Tenuki. A Japanese go term adopted into English that denotes playing elsewhere, especially breaking off from a sequence that remains to be resolved.
  • Advanced Terms
    • Aji. A weakness that is left behind in the opponent's position. Has been translated as flavor, aftertaste, and "funny business". Typically it can be exploited in more than one way.
    • Tesuji. An astute, often counter-intuitive tactical play that optimally exploits a defect in the opposing shapes.
    • Connection. A play that joins two stones or chains into a single chain by connecting them along the lines of the board; or that makes it possible for them to be so joined even if the opponent were to play first; or that makes it unprofitable for the opponent to actively separate them.
    • Miai. Two moves that have equivalent effects, such that if either player plays one, his opponent will play the other.
    • Moyo. A territorial framework, an extensive area loosely bounded by one player's stones, where the other has yet to establish any defensible positions, and which consequently could become the former's territory.
    • Hane. A single stone that "reaches around" the outside of an opposing unit diagonally, adjacent but unconnected to an existing unit.
    • Seki. A Japanese go term adopted into English, meaning an impasse in which stones are alive without two eyes because the opponent cannot or should not capture them. Also known as mutual life.
    • Tengen. The point in the very middle of the board.
    • Shape. Relative positions of stones of one color in close proximity.
    • Infuence. The effect stones exert at a distance.
  • Additional Terms
    • Capturing race. A race to fill in the liberties of two groups, neither of which can live independently. Also called semeai.
    • Dragon. A dragon is a long connected shape spanning large areas of the board.
    • Endgame. The final stage of the game.
    • Handicap. Stones that Black (the weaker player) places on the board before White's first move to ensure a more balanced contest.
    • Invasion. Play made inside an enemy framework with the intention of living or escaping.
