Comprehensive Rules of the board game Go

General

  1. Go is a board game for two players.
  2. One player plays black stones and the other player plays white stones. Players decide prior to the beginning of the game which player plays what color of stone. The players are also called Black and White.

Board

  1. A game of Go is played on a Go board. The Go board is a rectangular board that usually consists of 19 horizontal and 19 vertical straight lines forming a grid with 361 intersections.
  2. Other sizes of Go boards may be used as well: 9x9 and 13x13 are very common.
  3. Virtually all Go games are played on a square board; however, playing on a non-square board is also possible.
  4. The minimum size of a board is 2x2. The maximum size permitted by online servers is 25x25.
  5. Intersections on the Go board have coordinates.
  1. The up-down coordinate that determines the row is indicated by a number from 1 to 25. On computer displays, usually 1 is on the bottom.
  2. The left-right coordinate that determines the column of the intersection is indicated by a letter from A to Z. The letter “I” is skipped for historical reasons (with some fonts, it’s possible to confuse it with “J”). On computer displays, usually A is on the left.
  3. For example, the topright corner of a 25x25 board would have the coordinates Z25.

Object of the game

  1. The object of the game is to have more points than your opponent when the game ends. There are a number of different rulesets which differ by how points are awarded. To see how points are awarded, look further down in the Counting Phase section.
  2. Whoever has the most points when the game ends wins the game.
  3. In certain rare conditions, if additional compensation points are not given to the White player, it is possible that both players will have the same number of points when the game ends. In that case, the game ends in a draw.

Starting the game

At the start of the game, the players must agree on a number of choices.

  1. At the start of a game, the players first agree on which ruleset will the game use. This program supports Chinese rules, Japanese rules and AGA rules.
  2. The players agree on which player plays black stones and which player plays white stones.
  1. In tournaments, this may be decided by tournament rules.
  2. In online ranked play, this may be decided by the server’s balancing mechanism.
  3. Usually, the weaker player should play black stones.
  4. The traditional way to decide who goes first among players of the same strength is nigiri.
  1. To choose by traditional nigiri, the older player grabs a handful of white stones in his fist but doesn’t show the number of stones pickes to the the other player.
  2. The other player then either places two black stones on the board or one black stone to indicate whether he guess the first player picked an even or odd number of stones.
  3. The first player then places the stones in his hand on the board, arranging them in pairs so that the parity can be easily seen.
  4. If the second player guessed correctly, then the second player plays black stones, otherwise the first player plays black stones.
  5. Then remove all stones from the board.
  1. In online nonranked play, the challenger usually picks what color of stones they want to play.
  1. Then the players agree on the number of “komi compensation points” awarded automatically to White. These points are added to the score of the player playing white stones.
  1. The number of compensation points is usually between 5 and 8, the exact number varies by ruleset or tournament and changes in response to research. Some values are given later in this document, in the section Standard Compensation Points.
  2. If one player is stronger than the other player, they may award the other player additional compensation points to give them a better chance at victory. In such games, the number may even be 60 or 80, for example, and is thus usually awarded to Black. This is called “reverse Komi”.
  1. The player also decide on what time control, if any, they wish to use for the game. See the section on Time Control for details.
  2. Finally, the players decide on how many handicap stones Black should receive. Then these handicap stones are placed on the board.
  1. The game may use the free handicap stone placement option or it may use standard handicap stone placement.
  2. In free handicap stone placement, Black may place their stones on any intersections they wish.
  3. In standard handicap stone placement, stones are placed on positions determined by rules or tradition. The exact position for various board sizes is given in the section Standard Handicap Stone Placement. For some board sizes, there is no agreed-upon fixed position. For these board sizes, only free handicap stone placement may be used.
  1. When all of these decisions are made, clear all non-handicap stones from the board, each player taks the bowl with stones of their color and then Black may begin the game by making the first move.
  1. If any handicap stones were placed, instead White makes the first move after the placement of handicap stones.

Taking turns

  1. Players take turns in an alternating fashion. Black takes the first turn, then White.
  1. If any handicap stones were placed, instead White makes the first move after the placement of handicap stones.
  1. During your turn, you may either play a stone or pass. This is called “making a move”.
  1. To play a stone, place a stone of your color onto any empty intersection where it is permissible for you to play a stone.
  2. To pass, say that you pass.
  1. In the AGA ruleset, when you pass, you give one stone of your color to your opponent as a prisoner (do not take it from the board). This has the net effect of giving your opponent 1 point if territory scoring is used, and it has no effect if area scoring is used.
  1. If there is no empty intersection on the board where it is permissible for you to play a stone, you must pass.
  1. After you play a stone or pass, your turn ends and the other player takes their next turn.
  2. When both players pass in succession, the game proceeds to the Life/Death Determination Phase.
  1. In the AGA ruleset, the game proceeds to the Life/Death Determination Phase only after both players pass in succession, with White making the last pass. Therefore, it may be that three passes happen successively before the game proceeds to the next phase.
  1. A player may resign at any time. A player who resigns loses the game.

Definitions

  1. An intersection is connected to all adjacent intersections that are connected to it by a straight line. Thus, an intersection might have 2, 3 or 4 other intersections it is connected to (corner intersections have 2, side intersections have 3, other intersections have 4).
  2. A stone is connected to all stones of the same color that can be reached from that stone only by following by connected intersections occupied by stones of the same color.
  3. The set of all stones that a stone is connected to, including itself, is that stone’s group.
  4. All empty intersections adjacent to at least one stone of a group are called that group’s liberties.

Playing a stone & Capturing

  1. When you play a stone, first check whether your move captures one or more enemy groups. Your move captures a group of the other color if, after your stone is placed on the board, that group has no liberty.
  2. Then, for each group that your move captured, remove all stones of that group from the board. These stones become your “prisoners”.
  3. Finally, check whether the move you just made was a permissible play. If it wasn’t, it was not a legal play and you must take it back and do something else on your turn.

Permissible plays

  1. A full board position means complete information about a board, i.e. what intersections are occupied and by stones of which color; however, information about which player played last or which player is about to take their turn is not part of a full board position.
  2. If the “superko forbidden” rules option is used, it is not permissible to play a stone if the full board position that would result after that play (after any possible captured stones are removed) has already appeared before in the game. Such a move would be called an “illegal Ko move”.
  3. If the “superko forbidden” rules option is not used, then only simple Ko moves are illegal - a simple Ko move is one that would repeat the immediately preceding full board position. If an earlier full board position were to be repeated, it is for the referee to decide what happens. The possible outcomes are considering the game a draw, allowing the repetition, forbidding the repetition, voiding the game, replaying the game, or agreement among the players.
  4. It is not permissible to play a stone if that play would not cause any capture and in addition, the played stone would become part of a group that would have no liberties.
  5. All other plays are permitted.

Life/Death Determination Phase

  1. When both players pass in succession (with a cave-at for AGA rules, see Taking turns), the game proceeds to Life/Death Determination Phase.
  2. In this phase, both players determine which stones are alive and which are dead. In theory, the characteristics “alive” and “dead” are arbitrary markers. Players should agree on which stones are alive and dead.
  1. In Japanese rules, “alive” and “dead” are not arbitrary and are decided by the rules. However, these rules are complex and not well understood by most players. Full details of these rules are not yet part of this document.
  1. If players agree, then all stones marked dead are removed from the board and become prisoners of the player who didn’t place those stones. The game then ends.
  2. If players don’t agree, then gameplay is resumed. The opponent of the player who passed last makes a move first.
  1. Some rules specify additional constraints on how gameplay works after this point, and who plays first. However, these additional constraints only very rarely come into play and are not yet part of this document.

Counting Score

  1. When the game ends, each player’s score is counted. The player with the higher score wins the game. If both players tie for the best score, the game is a draw. Each player’s score is composed of several elements.
  2. If a player has any compensation points, these are added to their score.
  3. If the “area scoring” option is used:
  1. For each stone of a player’s color that’s still on the board, add 1 point to that player’s score.
  2. For each empty contiguous region of empty points, if this region is entirely surround by stones of only a single color (and possibly the edge of the game board), add 1 point for each point in the region to the player who plays this color.
  3. Other empty points don’t add points to either player.
  1. If the “territory scoring” option is used:
  1. For each empty contiguous region of empty points, if this region is entirely surround by stones of only a single color (and possibly the edge of the game board), add 1 point for each point in the region to the player who plays this color.
  2. Other empty points and any non-empty points don’t add points to either player.
  3. For each prisoner a player has, add 1 point to that player’s score.

Time Control

There are several time control options in Go. The most common ones are Japanese byoyomi (used in the Hikaru no Go and the Deepmind Challenge match) and Canadian byoyomi (used as a primary system on Pandanet). Canadian byoyomi is also called “Pandanet style” on Pandanet.

Fundamentally, all time control systems work like chess clocks: each player has a separate time limit and the time alloted to a player only ticks down when it’s that player’s turn. The details differ from system to system.

A player who runs out of time loses the game immediately.

In Canadian byoyomi, each player has a number of minutes as main time. When that elapses, the player enters their byoyomi period in which they have 10 minutes to make 25 moves (these are standard numbers, but the players may agree on different time limits). If a player uses up all ten minutes before making their 25th turn, they lose. When the player makes the 25th move, they enter another byoyomi period and regain their full 10 minutes to make 25 more turns (any unspent time from the previous period is lost).

In Japanese byoyomi, player has a number of minutes as main time. When that elapses, the player enters overtime. Each players has a number of byoyomi periods (usually 5) and those periods have a time limit each (usually 30 seconds). The first byoyomi period begins to tick down. If the player makes a move within it (i.e. in 30 seconds or less), then the period is not spent and next time, the player will use the same byoyomi period. They can do this indefinitely. However, if 30 seconds elapse without the player making a move, that byoyomi period is “spent” and cannot be recovered. The player immediately enters their second byoyomi period and has 30 more seconds to make a move, and so on. When the player spends all 5 periods, they lose the game.

Rulesets

The three most common rulesets are the Japanese rules, the Chinese rules and the AGA rules. These are differences between the rules:

  • Chinese rules
  • Superko forbidden. Repeating any full board position is forbidden.
  • Area scoring. Area counts for score, not territory.
  • AGA rules
  • Superko forbidden. Repeating any full board position is forbidden.
  • Territory or area scoring. Both scoring options are possible.
  • White passes last. The last move of the game must be a pass by White.
  • Pass stones. When you pass, your opponent receives an extra prisoner.
  • Japanese rules
  • Territory scoring. Territory counts for score, not area.

Note that online servers may not implement these rules precisely. For example, the may allow superko repetitions that would normally be forbidden, or they may cause a different player to take the first turn after gameplay is resumed from the Life/Death Determination Phase.

Standard Handicap Stone Placement

The handicap stones are traditionally played as follows:

  • 2 stones on the 1st and 2nd star points
  • 3 stones on the 1st through 3rd star points
  • 4 stones on the 1st through 4th star points
  • 5 stones on the 1st through 4th star points and the center point
  • 6 stones on the 1st through 6th star points
  • 7 stones on the 1st through 6th star points and the center point
  • 8 stones on the 1st through 8th star points
  • 9 stones on the 1st through 9th star points

Star points are at the following coordinates:

  • 1st star point Q-16
  • 2nd star point D-4
  • 3rd star point Q-4
  • 4th star point D-16
  • 5th star point Q-10
  • 6th star point D-10
  • 7th star point K-16
  • 8th star point K-4
  • 9th star point K-10 (center point)

Usually, on a standard 19x19 board, Black should place as many handicap stones as there is rank difference between players. If, for example, Black is rank 14k and White is rank 13k, Black should place 4 handicap stones. In handicap games, Black is always the weaker player.

If the handicap is “1 stone”, no handicap stones are placed, but the compensation points for White are decreased to 0 or 0.5 instead of their standard value, and Black still goes first.

Standard Compensation Points

  1. Under Japanese rules, 6.5 compensation points are given to White.
  2. Under Chinese rules, 7.5 compensation points are given to White.
  3. Under AGA rules, 7.5 compensation points are given to White, regardless of the scoring system used.
  4. If area scoring is used (either in Chinese or AGA rules), 1 additional compensation point is given to White for each Black handicap stone byeond the first.
